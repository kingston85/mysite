Teaching Approach

Pedagogical methods that guide my educational practice

Experiential Learning

I believe students learn best by doing. My courses incorporate field work, laboratory experiments, and real-world projects that allow students to apply theoretical knowledge to practical challenges. These hands-on experiences develop technical skills while reinforcing conceptual understanding.

Collaborative Learning

Environmental challenges require interdisciplinary teams and diverse perspectives. I structure group projects and discussions to help students develop collaboration skills, learn from each other's experiences, and practice consensus building—essential skills for future environmental professionals.

Problem-Based Learning

My courses are structured around real environmental challenges facing Liberia and West Africa. By tackling authentic problems, students develop critical thinking skills, learn to integrate knowledge from multiple disciplines, and become more engaged with course material.

Local Context Integration

I emphasize the importance of local ecological knowledge, community perspectives, and cultural values in environmental management. My courses incorporate case studies from Liberia and invite community experts to share their insights, ensuring that classroom learning is relevant to local realities.

Active Learning

I minimize traditional lecture formats in favor of interactive activities that engage students as active participants in the learning process. Discussions, debates, case analyses, and student presentations develop communication skills and deeper understanding of complex concepts.

Growth Mindset

I create a supportive classroom environment that encourages intellectual risk-taking and views mistakes as learning opportunities. Regular formative assessments and constructive feedback help students track their progress and continuously improve throughout the course.

Environmental Soil Chemistry

ENV 305

This course examines the chemical processes occurring in soils and their influence on plant growth, water quality, nutrient cycling, and contaminant behavior. Special emphasis is placed on soil remediation techniques and the management of contaminated sites in tropical environments.

Learning Objectives:

  • Understand fundamental soil chemical properties and processes
  • Analyze the behavior of nutrients and contaminants in soil systems
  • Apply appropriate sampling and analytical techniques for soil assessment
  • Evaluate soil remediation strategies for various types of contamination
  • Develop management plans for contaminated soil in Liberian contexts

Field Methods in Environmental Science

ENV 245

This intensive field course provides hands-on training in environmental monitoring and assessment techniques. Students develop practical skills in sample collection, field analysis, data management, and quality assurance while conducting environmental assessments in diverse ecosystems around Monrovia.

Learning Objectives:

  • Master fundamental field sampling techniques for various environmental media
  • Operate common field monitoring equipment and portable analytical tools
  • Implement proper sample handling, preservation, and chain-of-custody procedures
  • Apply appropriate quality assurance and quality control measures
  • Interpret field data and recognize potential sources of error

Course Structure:

  • Field Sessions: Monday through Thursday, 8:00 AM - 3:00 PM
  • Laboratory Analysis: Fridays, 9:00 AM - 12:00 PM

Academic Teaching

Educating the next generation of environmental scientists and sustainable development professionals

Teaching Philosophy

My approach to teaching is founded on the belief that environmental education must bridge theory and practice, empowering students to become change agents in their communities. I strive to create an inclusive learning environment where students actively engage with complex environmental challenges through critical thinking and hands-on experiences.

"Environmental education should not only inform but transform — creating leaders who can navigate complex ecological challenges with both scientific rigor and cultural sensitivity."

In my classroom, I emphasize the interdisciplinary nature of environmental science, helping students understand the interconnections between scientific, social, economic, and ethical dimensions of environmental issues. This holistic approach is particularly important in the Liberian context, where sustainable development requires solutions that are both scientifically sound and culturally appropriate.

I believe in the power of experiential learning, incorporating field trips, laboratory work, and community-based projects into my courses whenever possible. These experiences help students develop practical skills while also fostering a deeper connection to the environmental challenges facing their communities.

As an educator, I am committed to mentoring students beyond the classroom, supporting their research interests and professional development. I find particular fulfillment in helping students discover their own passions within the broad field of environmental science and guiding them toward meaningful careers that contribute to Liberia's sustainable future.
