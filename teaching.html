Journal Article 2024

Life Cycle Assessment of Construction and Demolition Waste Management Strategies in Urban West Africa

Emmanuel, S., Wong, L., Chen, J., & Kamara, M.

Journal of Sustainable Development, 12(3), 145-162.

This study applies life cycle assessment methodology to evaluate the environmental impacts of alternative management strategies for construction and demolition waste in urban centers of West Africa, with Monrovia, Liberia as a case study. Three scenarios were assessed: landfilling (baseline), partial recycling, and advanced recycling with material recovery. Results indicate that the advanced recycling scenario reduces global warming potential by 42% and cumulative energy demand by 37% compared to landfilling. However, challenges related to technology access, infrastructure limitations, and economic feasibility were identified as critical barriers to implementation. The study proposes a phased approach to transition from current practices to more sustainable waste management systems, considering the specific socioeconomic context of developing urban areas in West Africa.

Journal Article 2023

Comparative Analysis of Heavy Metal Contamination in Urban Soils of Monrovia: Implications for Environmental Health

Emmanuel, S., Liu, Y., & Johnson, T.

Environmental Monitoring and Assessment, 195(4), 231-249.

This research investigated the levels of heavy metal contamination in urban soils across different land use types in Monrovia, Liberia. Soil samples were collected from 32 sites representing residential, commercial, industrial, and recreational areas. Analysis revealed elevated concentrations of lead, cadmium, and chromium in industrial and commercial zones, exceeding WHO safety standards by 2-4 times. Spatial distribution analysis identified three contamination hotspots associated with historical industrial activities. Risk assessment indicated moderate to high ecological risk, particularly for children in residential areas adjacent to industrial zones. The study provides baseline data for urban soil quality in Monrovia and recommends targeted remediation strategies and policy interventions to address contamination issues and protect public health.

Journal Article 2022

Carbon Footprint Assessment of Municipal Solid Waste Management in Developing Urban Centers: Case Study of Monrovia

Emmanuel, S., Wang, H., & Smith, K.

Waste Management Research, 40(2), 118-133.

This paper presents a comprehensive carbon footprint assessment of the municipal solid waste management system in Monrovia, Liberia. Using the IPCC methodology and primary data collected over 12 months, the study quantified greenhouse gas emissions across the waste management chain, from collection to final disposal. Results showed that open dumping and uncontrolled decomposition accounted for 78% of total emissions, primarily methane. A scenario analysis of alternative waste management strategies demonstrated that implementing a combination of composting for organic waste and sanitary landfilling with gas capture could reduce emissions by up to 65%. Cost-benefit analysis indicated that while initial investment costs are substantial, operational costs over a 10-year period would be lower than current practices, with significant environmental co-benefits. The study provides policymakers with quantitative evidence to support sustainable waste management planning in similar urban contexts in developing countries.

Conference Paper 2023

Integrating Indigenous Knowledge and Modern Science in Soil Remediation: Perspectives from West Africa

Emmanuel, S., & Kamara, J.

Proceedings of the International Conference on Environmental Science and Technology, Barcelona, Spain, pp. 412-425.

This paper explores the potential synergies between indigenous knowledge systems and modern scientific approaches in soil remediation techniques applicable to West African contexts. Through a review of existing practices and field studies in rural Liberia, the research identifies several traditional methods of soil treatment that demonstrate effectiveness in addressing specific contaminants. These include the use of certain plant species for phytoremediation, specific clay application techniques, and seasonal burning practices. Laboratory analysis confirmed the efficacy of selected traditional methods, particularly for organic contaminants. The paper proposes an integrated framework that leverages the strengths of both knowledge systems to develop culturally appropriate, cost-effective, and environmentally sustainable remediation strategies for resource-constrained settings.

Conference Paper 2022

Challenges and Opportunities in Implementing Circular Economy Principles in Liberia's Construction Sector

Emmanuel, S., Zhang, L., & Cooper, R.

Circular Economy in Developing Nations Conference, Nairobi, Kenya, pp. 87-101.

This paper examines the potential for implementing circular economy principles in Liberia's rapidly growing construction sector. Through stakeholder interviews, site visits, and material flow analysis, the research identifies key barriers including limited regulatory frameworks, knowledge gaps, market challenges for recycled materials, and infrastructural constraints. Despite these challenges, several opportunities emerge, including the growing awareness among key stakeholders, potential for job creation, reduced environmental impacts, and conservation of limited resources. The paper proposes a staged implementation framework that addresses immediate waste management concerns while building capacity for longer-term circular economy transitions. Policy recommendations focus on creating enabling conditions through regulatory measures, economic incentives, education, and public-private partnerships tailored to Liberia's socioeconomic realities.
